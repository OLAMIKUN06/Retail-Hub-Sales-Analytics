In[33]:
# Months in 'anomalies' may indicate special events, issues, or opportunities.

monthly_sales = sales.groupby('Month')['totalSalesAfterDiscount'].sum()
print(monthly_sales)

# ==============================
# Anomaly detection in monthly sales
# ==============================

# Calculate mean and standard deviation of monthly sales
mean = monthly_sales.mean()
std = monthly_sales.std()

print("\nAverage Monthly Sales:", round(mean, 2))
print("\nStandard Deviation of Monthly Sales:", round(std, 2))

# Define anomalies: months where sales are much higher or lower than usual
# Here we use 2 standard deviations from the mean as the threshold
anomalies = monthly_sales[
    (monthly_sales > mean + 2 * std) | (monthly_sales < mean - 2 * std)
]

print("\nAnomalies (months with unusually high or low sales):")
print('\n',anomalies)

Insights

1. Discount vs Total Sales After Discount (Scatter Plot) Most of the sales are concentrated at 0.00 discount, with total sales after discount mostly between 0 and 2000. At 0.10 discount, there are only 3 sales points, with total sales around 500, 1100 and 1750. At 0.20 discount, there are about 6 sales points, with totals around 0, 200, 250, 300 and 1300. This suggests that the store mostly sells products without any discount, and when discounts are applied, they are relatively few and spread across different sales amounts. There is no clear strong pattern that higher discount always leads to much higher sales, at least from this small sample. Insight: The business relies heavily on full-price sales (0% discount), and discounts are used rarely and not in a clearly structured way.2. Total Sales vs ProductID (Top 10 products) Product with ProductID 369 has the highest total sales of about 2000 among the top 10 products. Products with ProductID 2 and ProductID 147 have the lowest total sales among the top 10, both around 1250. The difference between the highest (2000) and the lowest (1250) in the top 10 is noticeable, but not extremely huge, meaning sales are somewhat distributed, but product 369 stands out. Insight: Product 369 is the best-performing product in terms of total sales, while products 2 and 147 are on the lower end among the top 10.3. Total Sales vs CustomerID (Top 10 customers) CustomerID 87577 has the highest total sales of about 2000 among the top 10 customers. CustomerID 51149 and CustomerID 91382 have the lowest total sales among the top 10, both around 1250. Just like products, one customer (87577) clearly contributes more revenue than the others in the top 10. Insight: A small group of key customers drives a large share of revenue, with customer 87577 being a high-value customer.4. Monthly Sales Trend After Discount (Line Chart) The highest monthly sales occur in April, with total sales after discount of about 14,000. The lowest monthly sales occur in January, with around 2,500 in sales. From January to April, sales seem to increase strongly, then slightly decrease in May (based on your anomaly data). Insight: There is a strong positive trend in the early months of the year, with sales growing from January and peaking in April. Insight: Although April is clearly the highest month, it is still considered within a normal range statistically. The sales pattern shows strong growth and some variation, but no month is extremely abnormal from a statistical anomaly perspective.

Recommendations

Based on these insights, here are practical recommendations you can use: 1. Leverage high-performing products and categories Focus on promoting ProductID 369 and CategoryID 1 through marketing, bundling, or cross-selling, since they are already strong performers. Ensure these products/categories are always in stock. 2. Review low-performing products and categories Investigate why ProductID 2, ProductID 147 and CategoryID 8 have relatively low sales. Consider promotions, discounts, product repositioning, or even discontinuation if they are not profitable. 3. Segment and reward high-value customers Create a special strategy for CustomerID 87577 and other top customers (e.g., loyalty rewards, early access to new products, exclusive offers). This can help retain them and possibly increase their lifetime value. 4. Use discounts more strategically Since most sales happen at 0% discount, the business is not fully leveraging pricing as a tool. Test structured discount campaigns (for example, 1020% off during low months like January) to see if it significantly boosts sales. 5. Plan around seasonal trends Sales peak in April and are lowest in January. Use January for inventory checks, planning, and targeted promotions to boost demand. For months like April, ensure sufficient stock, staffing, and marketing to maximize revenue. 6. Monitor small vs large transactions Since high sales amounts are rare, track these big orders carefully. Consider offering premium services or follow-ups for large transactions, because losing those big customers could hurt revenue.

Limitations

Its important to be honest about the limitations of the analysis: 1. Limited time period The trend and anomaly analysis is based only on data from January to May 2018 (5 months), which is a short period for drawing very strong seasonal conclusions. 2. Data quality issues (ID mismatches) There were mismatched IDs between Sales and Customers / Products tables in the original SQL data, which affected joins. Some relationships between customers, sales, and products may not have been fully analyzed because of this. 3. Sample size and aggregation Some visualizations (like scatter plot of Discount vs Sales) are based on a small number of records per discount level, which makes patterns less clear. The analysis mostly uses aggregated values (top 10 products/customers, monthly totals), which hides some detailed behavior at transaction level. 4. No cost or profit data The analysis is based on sales amounts only, without considering cost, profit margin, or returns. A product with high sales may not be the most profitable if its costs are high. 5. Assumptions on discount behavior Discounts are treated as simple percentage discounts, and the impact is not tested with advanced models (like regression or causal analysis).

Conclusion

This project demonstrates how SQL and Python can be combined to move from raw transactional data to business insights. Using SQL, the data was cleaned and prepared (calculations for total sales before and after discount, joins with product and category tables, etc.). Then, in Python, the analysis focused on: Understanding how discounts relate to sales Identifying top products and customers Exploring sales distribution Comparing performance across product categories Analyzing monthly sales trends Checking for anomalies in sales patterns Overall, the analysis shows that: The business relies strongly on full-price sales with minimal discount usage. A few products, categories, and customers contribute a significant share of total revenue. Sales grow strongly from January to April, indicating a possible seasonal pattern or successful activities in those months. Despite variation, no extreme anomalies were detected in monthly performance using a 2-standard-deviation rule. With more complete data (including costs, longer time periods, and cleaner IDs), this analysis could be extended into profitability analysis, customer segmentation, and predictive modeling, which would further strengthen its value for decision-making.
